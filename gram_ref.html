1: Introduction

Theroan, in itself known as Nam't S'dal'am, is a conlang (constructed language) created by me, Henry, for a fantasy universe which I am creating.

Theroan serves no purpose other than in-universe worldbuilding.

2: Phonology

Theroan has 22 distinct phonemes which can each be represented by the Latin alphabet.

Note: the following is written using the International Phonetic Alphabet (not to be confused with the International Phonetic Association).

2.1: Vowels

Theroan has 5 vowels:

Front Central Back
Close i u
Mid ε o
Open a

2.2: Consonants

Theroan has 17 vowels:

Note: 'w' represents the voiced labial-velar approximant, though it is marked as the voiced velar approximant (which is 'ɰ')

Several characters in the IPA are not standard Latin characters. Thus, there are several transliterations:

2.4: Stress

Stress is always on the penultimate (second-to-last) syllable.

3: Morphology

Theroan has the following word classes:

  • Nouns (n.)
  • Verbs (v.)
  • Adjectives (adj.)
  • Adverbs (adv.)
  • Pronouns (pron.)
  • Adpositions (adp.)
  • Conjunctions (conj.)
  • Interjection (interj.)

3.1: Nouns

Theroan nouns are inflected for number, possession, and the vocative case.

3.1.1: Number

Grammatical number in Theroan has singular and plural forms. Unmarked nouns are singular, while the prefix mal- indicates plurality.

Number Theroan English
Singular (sg) sal animal
Plural (pl) malsal animals

3.1.2: Possession

Theroan uses a possessor-possessee system, in which one noun is a possessor, marked with the suffix -'t, and another noun is the possessee (unmarked):

Possession Theroan English
Possessor (gen) mel't his
Possessee mel he/him

For example:

Nem't

person-gen

rem

cat

awtan'lad

jump-pst.pfv

The person's cat jumped.

3.1.3: Vocative Case

In the vocative case, which refers to the addressee, use the suffix -'o:

Jon'o

John-voc

awtan'lad!

jump-pst.pfv

You jumped, John!

3.2: Verbs

Verbs are conjugated for tense. There are 2 valency-changing operators, as well.

3.2.1: Tense

Theroan uses 6 tenses: present perfective, present imperfective, past perfective, past imperfective, future perfective, and future imperfective. Each has their own conjugation:

Perfective Imperfective
Present ∅ -'rit (prs.npfv)
Past -'lad (pst.pfv) -'rad (pst.npfv)
Future -'lak (fut.pfv) -'rak (fut.npfv)

3.2.2: Passive Valency Operator

The passive valency operator r'dal makes a transitive sentence into an intransitive sentence; transitive sentences take in 2 objects, and intransitive sentences take in 1. It comes after all other words in a sentence. This has the effect of creating the passive voice:

Nan

1sg

man

2sg

pilnas.

see

I see you.

versus

(Nan)

(1sg)

man

2sg

pilnas

see

r'dal.

pass

You are seen (by me).

3.2.3: Causative Valency Operator

In reverse to the passive valency operator, the causative valency operator ten changes an intransitive sentence into a transitive sentence; thus, a subject which was not present before must be added. This has the effect of "making" the subject do the verb:

Nan

1sg

awtan.

jump

I jump.

versus

Nan

1sg

man

2sg

awtan

jump

ten.

caus

I make you jump.

3.3: Modifiers

Modifiers (i.e. adjectives and adverbs) come directly before nouns and verbs.

3.3.1: Comparative and Superlative Forms

Theroan has a comparative and superlative form as suffixes:

Suffix En. equivalent
Comparative -jo (cmpr) -er
Superlative -jor (sup) -est

3.4.1: Personal Pronouns

4: Syntax

4.1: Word Order

The base word order is S-O-V (subject-object-verb):

Nan

1sg

tip

wine

tseth'lad

get-pst.pfv

I got the wine.

4.2: Adpositions

Any adposition (in Theroan postposition) is added directly after any noun or verb and can contain a clause:

Nan

1sg

s'dal'lak

speak-fut.pfv

t'alal

to

man.

2sg

I will speak to you.

There can also be multiple adpositions to one verb:

Nan

1sg

s'dal'lak

speak-fut.pfv

t'alal

to

man

2sg

theb

about

tel.

prox

I will talk to you about this.

Note that an adposition can come after subjects and objects, as well.

4.3: Y/N Questions

Yes/no questions are formed in Theroan by using the interrogative auxiliary wuk, which is a primary auxiliary verb. Primary auxiliaries come after the adposition but before modal auxiliaries:

Nan

1sg

man

2sg

pilnes

know

wuk?

int

Do I know you?

4.4: Wh-questions

Wh-questions use the interrogative pronoun tsum, equivalent to the English "what." It can also be used as a determiner, i.e. "what-person" or "what-time," as substitutes for "who" or "when:"

Tel

prox

tsum

wh

selal?

cop

What is this?


Mel

3sg

t'agal'lak

travel-fut.pfv

sujal

at

tsum

what

nemk?

time

When will he travel?

4.5: Modality

Modality in Theroan is expressed through modal auxiliaries. Modal auxiliaries come before valency operators but after primary auxiliaries.

Here are some examples of moods and their corresponding modal auxiliaries (indicative is unmarked):

Mood Modal Aux. Meaning
Imperative (imp) sujar Command
Jussive (jus) asu'an Exhortation
Subjunctive (sjv) thunam Possibilities
Permissive (perm) saner What one is allowed to do
Obligative (oblg) menak What one must do
Abilitive (abil) jawl What one is able to do
Desiderative (des) ants What one desires to do
Negative (neg) lewal What won't happen

As an example:

Mel

3sg

awtan

jump

wuk

int

menak?

oblg

Should he jump?

Note that you can also use multiple modal auxiliaries in one sentence.

4.6: Subordinate Clauses

Theroan has 2 kinds of subordinate clauses: relative and content.

4.6.1: Relative Clauses

Relative clauses are separate clauses which are "incomplete," i.e. missing either the subject or object.

Relative clauses are denoted by the relativizing conjunction. They are placed at the beginning of a relative clause.

The first type of relativizing conjunction is the nominative one. This means that the object of the clause is the noun which the clause is built off of, so the object is not said. For instance, view the following phrase:

k'dal

book

sumat

rel.nom

nan

1sg

kuben'lad

read-pst.pfv

the book that I read

The relative clause in its whole means 'I read the book,' but since it is in the nominative case, the object is left out.

Likewise, there is an accusative case, where the subject is left out:

n'awr

yellow

an

place

sutem

rel.acc

malk'dal

pl-book

nathur

make

the yellow place that makes books

4.6.2: Content Clauses

Content clauses are different from relative clauses in that they contain complete clauses. They use the subordinator emtsath to mark off the beginning of a content clause:

Nan

1sg

emtsath

sub

man

2sg

t'agal'lad

travel-pst.pfv

pil'lad

think-pst.pfv

I thought that you had traveled.

Note: the previous sentence did not contain an object, but this is because of 'travel' being an intransitive verb; you can still use subjects and objects in content clauses.
